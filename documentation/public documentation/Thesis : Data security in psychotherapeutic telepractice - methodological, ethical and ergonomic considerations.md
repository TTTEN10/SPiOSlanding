## 1. Part one: Historical overview

### 1.1. Historical context
In a historical context, the first ethical and deontological approaches in telepsychology were introduced in the early 2000s (Leroux, 2008).
The rapid growth of online platforms dedicated to online therapy […] contributes to the reorganisation of challenges related to mental health and is an indicator of it. (Brossard et al., 2013).
During the 2010s, teleconsultation increased significantly through platforms offering their mental health services online.
> « The practices of psychologists on the net, in particular video consultation, will develop and become more professional in correlation with the evolution of social uses and ICT (Information and Communication Technology). » (Haddouk et al., 2013)
After nearly fifteen years of research, [studies] have demonstrated the effectiveness of online psychotherapies for cases of depression, phobia(s) and anxiety disorders. (Watts et al., 2016)

### 1.2. Popularisation and democratisation of telepsychology
In autumn 2019, the FFPP (Fédération Française des Psychologues – French Federation of Psychologists) expressed the wish to hold a conference devoted to cyberpsychology, noting that the stakes of telepsychology are numerous and important. The ethical theme was concluded as follows:

> « It is important to be able both to encourage these new practices and to be vigilant and realistic about their limits, [...], particularly from an ethical standpoint »

The COVID crisis propelled telepsychology to the forefront of psychologists’ practice themes from spring 2020 onwards, with the preparation of the initial conference leading the FFPP to react quickly.
In the post-COVID period, different approaches to the possible evolution of telepsychology helped to highlight a consensus that could be summarised as a discipline destined to develop: The processes put in place for the progression of this discipline will need to operate through contributions from research and practice experts, to become a useful and well-defined complement to the practices of psychologists. This will improve understanding of the use of these tools by professionals and consequently better quality of care for patients and users of telepsychology services. From then on, the theme of remote care became a priority, and there was discussion of the major social changes linked to the pandemic that had a direct impact on patients’ mental health and psychologists’ practices.

#### 1.2.1. Professional practice analysis: stakes, advantages and limits
« Professional Practice Analysis (APP) is a psychologically grounded intervention, scientifically and ethically, framed by theories of learning – training framework – and of the helping relationship both in its active principles (empathy, affective attunement, validation) and in its tools (unconditional listening, reflection, reformulation, etc.). »
Notions in telepsychology have accompanied reflection on the APP process:
It is necessary to improve patient care by understanding clinical situations and their stakes, taking into account professionals’ professional development needs through a scientifically valid and accessible technological framework for all. Individual and technological developments therefore appear closely linked.
The APA’s (2013) « Joint Task Force for the Development of Telepsychology » team emphasises that one of the key skills of an APP facilitator is to be able to evaluate and analyse the relevance of an APP framework in telepsychology. The facilitator must consider

scientific data, environmental and contextual factors to determine whether the technological framework is appropriate and thus its impact on participants.
In these evaluations and analyses between professionals, attention is paid in parallel to users and regulators who influence professional practices in telepsychology:
At the individual patient level, in a telepsychotherapy setting, the usual defence mechanisms (avoidance, rationalisation, derision, etc.) appear and combine in particular with: distrust of technology and fear of breach of confidentiality. (Tarlow et al., 2020), (Pierce et al., 2020)
At the institutional level, fear of loss of control is observed to go hand in hand with the temptation to breach confidentiality and ethics. (Brooks et al., 2013)
From these resistances we can see that on both sides, the major risk of drift and dysfunction is ethical; In this context we can identify at least two reasons against implementing an APP group in telepsychology: ethical and symbolic.
In this context, APP is a subject that still requires much research, in particular to prepare for new opportunities and challenges. (Maheu et al., 2012).
Mental health professionals generally have a favourable attitude towards online healthcare, but they have also reported obstacles to the implementation of this technology. Lack of knowledge about the effectiveness of online healthcare, technology-related concerns (data security, for example), as well as ethical, practical and contextual factors have been identified as barriers to implementing this approach. (Stallard et al., 2010), (Mayer et al., 2019).
Psychologists in collaboration with the European Federation of Psychologists’ Associations (EFPA) project group on online mental health have recently developed general guidance for mental health professionals, health services, regulatory bodies and developers to promote the creation and implementation of higher-quality online mental health interventions. (Van Daele et al., 2021). The EFPA initiated an online health project group in 2015 to develop a better understanding of the field and to devise a strategy for member associations.
In 2020, this « Project group » conducted an online survey to assess the use of online consultations in the context of the COVID-19 pandemic, to assess mental health professionals’ experiences and concerns with this treatment

modality, and to assess the overall effects of the transition to online mental health during the pandemic.
In total, 1,420 participants reported one or more concerns regarding current use of online consultations. Here are some results relevant to the research question:
Psychologist-related factors: A minority of participants (N = 16) indicated that lack of support from their organisations or associations was their main current concern.
Concerns about lack of resources needed for online consultations were common and related mainly to lack of knowledge or the wish to be better informed about online consultations (N = 148). A minority of participants were concerned about incompatibility of online consultation software with other systems or practices (N = 27) or lack of support in terms of technical assistance in case of system difficulties (N = 5). The need for additional training was greater in France (N = 48/250) than in Italy (N = 7/250).
Patient-related factors: Respondents reported being concerned about accessibility conditions for their patients (N = 230) and, to a lesser extent, lack of technical knowledge (N = 33). A small number of concerns were raised regarding data security (N = 14).
Contextual factors: The most frequently raised concern (more than 20% of the sample) related to the confidentiality and security of online consultation software (N = 442/2082). Professionals report concerns about the confidentiality and security of online consultation software as well as legal aspects or ethical standards.
However, they expressed a clear need for knowledge about psychological processes in online consultations, as well as about technical implementation aspects.
These perceived obstacles demonstrate the lack of relevant training of professionals in online consultations or e-mental health.

### 1.3. Evolution from recommendations to regulations

#### 1.3.1. The evolution of telepsychology since COVID-19
A major international survey on the use of telepsychology during the Covid-19 pandemic was set up. Recruitment took place in early 2020 through the mailing lists and social media of the European Federation of Psychologists’ Associations (EFPA) as well as national associations and project collaborators in different countries.
Training and its effects: Very few European countries reported specific training for psychologists: In Austria, Finland and the United Kingdom, more than one fifth of respondents had received specific training for teleconsultation.
In France, less than 5% of respondents had received prior training, and the same was true for 6.6% of respondents in Belgium.
We thus observe that countries where psychologists had little experience of online consultations before the Covid-19 outbreak are countries where specific training on online consultations was not disseminated to the professional community as a whole. Similarly, we observe that in these countries, online consultation was only moderately practised.
The challenge of adapting software to telepsychology practice: Along the same lines, the overall experience of online consultation is rated less favourably in the Netherlands and Sweden, where telepsychology practices have nevertheless expanded and where specific training has been widely provided. This therefore raises again the question of how the « digital tool » adapts to professionals and how to promote it.
It can be inferred that most psychologists had to start their practice without appropriate training, which supports the correlation between training and perception. In France, psychologists began online practice under difficult conditions during the first lockdown in 2020: with no prior experience or training in telepsychology.
This raises questions about guaranteeing a clinical and ethical framework and improvements to be considered for the future.
The first results of the survey conducted by the EFPA e-health group revealed a significant increase in online practices for psychologists in Europe but also a general lack of training in telepsychology before starting this practice. (De Witte et al., 2021)

The results of this study indicate that felt experience in practice plays a key role in the positive perception of teleconsultation. Felt experience in practice is linked to telepresence and a generally favourable opinion towards e-health practices. This result highlights a correlation between training in telepsychology and felt experience in practice in explaining the perception of the telepsychology session, which suggests that appropriate training in teleconsultation should improve telepresence and a generally positive felt experience in the practice of teleconsultation.
According to APA recommendations, « psychologists who provide telepsychology services strive to take reasonable steps to ensure their competence both with the technologies used and the potential impact of these technologies on clients/patients ». The French representatives at the EFPA note that: « These are certainly avenues to explore to strengthen the framework for telepsychology in France, including reflection on adaptations of the technological framework. »
In parallel, at the FFPP conference in January and March 2021, the approach to specific training of the Ordine degli Psicologi della Lombardia in Italy (OPL) was presented: The OPL, which represents approximately 20% of Italian psychologists, addressed early on the issues related to telepsychology practices arising from the new challenges of the modern global context. (Sara Gabbri, 2021) The Italians were very innovative regarding e-health training; indeed, the need to provide cutting-edge responses to Italian psychologists led the OPL to begin as early as 2012 to create a structured approach to adopting technology in clinical practice, reflecting the increase in interest in technological innovation in the field of psychology taking into account the avant-garde context in the international arena. The use of technology in clinical practice became an important subject in the following years, until 2019 when it became a structured and certified training theme for OPL members, offering practical and ethical guidance for the use of this tool. The aim was not only to update knowledge but also to create a space to discuss perceptions and obstacles related to the use of this technology.
A continuing education programme was also published in 2021 including literature updates and good practices in telepsychology.

1. The conceptual framework, providing a state of the art and overview of existing literature.
2. Examination of ethical and deontological issues.
3. Practical guidance. International research and proposals are also consulted as resources to offer practical guidance to colleagues for remote interventions.
Regarding what the professional must prepare in advance for teleconsultation interventions, on the different services that can be offered at a distance including suggestions on technologies to use, tool choices, payment system and questions to keep in mind.
It is important for professionals to prepare in advance for remote consultations, by finding out: Information and updates are provided on the different service options available, technologies to use, relevant tools, payment methods and considerations to take into account.
The OPL and Italian psychologists set an example of what could be used internationally by associations, federations and organisations in psychology.

### 1.4. Institutional regulation modes and roles of organisations
Psychologists' remote practices depend for their part on the broader legal framework of telehealth and have not so far been explicitly encouraged by the various bodies of the profession, which have maintained a cautious wait-and-see position; the framework for activity among psychologists is little constrained by the State and the profession is characterised by weak regulation of the professional group.
Professional organisations have therefore proved relatively neutral, but it should be recalled that their capacity for action is limited. They are nevertheless concerned by these developments.
The pandemic having caused a lack of information for practitioners, the FFPP and the SNP created a charter aimed at establishing clear relations between psychologists and teleconsultation platforms. This charter fits within a context of expert recommendations and the online dossier created by the FFPP was referenced by the Haute Autorité de Santé (HAS – French National Authority for Health) as a useful resource in the form of a "rapid response" fact sheet during the lockdown.
#### 1.4.1. A global approach to international standards
Regarding psychologists, France is engaged within the EFPA, having within and through this framework more substantial specialised institutional support to which it contributes, which falls under indicative and incentive standards:
● The « Project group eHealth » has worked on the development of detailed recommendations, addressing the different categories of stakeholders involved;
● Some of its members have participated in the eMEN project (e-Mental Health Innovation and Transnational Implementation Platform North-West Europe) launched to increase the dissemination and quality of online mental health services in Europe.
● The EFPA recommendations, formalised in 2006, revised and updated in 2021.
#### 1.4.2. Regulation logic
We are currently, regarding psychologists, at a stage of distant references to official good practice recommendations (formulated by the HAS), stemming from telemedicine, but the extension of teleconsultation practices and the multiplication of platforms could lead to a take-up of recommendations specific to the profession.
These should be based on feedback from field practice and fit within respect for and openness to the plurality of practices and in reference to the Code of deontology of psychologists, whether through an evolution of the code itself or of annexed provisions that could evolve more flexibly in a rapidly evolving field. The question of a training requirement for teleconsultations and/or a requirement to obtain accreditation has been considered.
We would then like to highlight the different recent recommendations and proposals formulated in France and in Europe, which are structured as complementary, regarding the different approaches to the characteristics of the adoption of technologies for psychotherapeutic purposes:

### 1.6. Practice and ethics on the internet according to the EFPA
#### 1.6.1. Regarding security
Of the psychologist: He or she must ensure that they are easily identifiable (professionally and geographically) as a qualified professional and member of an association/register.
Of the patient/client/user: He or she must be identifiable, even if anonymity may be appropriate in certain contexts.
#### 1.6.2. Regarding confidentiality
Recognition of limitations: Psychologists are recommended to inform users of any relevant legislation concerning data protection, records, communication of information and limits to confidentiality. They clarify with users the records they keep and the user's rights regarding them.
Record-keeping: Associations must advise their members to keep appropriate records (including backup copies) and to store them securely. Psychologists must be aware that recording and storage of information may be carried out by two (or three) parties.
#### 1.6.3. Regarding services via the internet
« 6. Recommendations and/or provisions for appropriate training on information technology, security aspects, special handling and caveats and limits concerning new methods, [...]. »
« 11. Arrangements are necessary for the secure holding and appropriate sharing of clinical data. Similarly, there should be safe and ethical procedures for the disposal of data when necessary. »
« 12. Member associations should consider supporting psychologists so that they are aware of relevant transnational ethical issues and legal regulations, where applicable. »
« 14. Psychologists should clearly separate private communication and accounts from professional communication in all their aspects (accounts, computers or profiles, address book, storage and so on). They should never use public computers for their service. »
« 15. Informed consent is necessary for storage and for any recording of the service by the client or the psychologist. »
The EFPA has also issued other considerations to be taken into account for member associations; in our view, it is important to retain the last two:
« 3. Associations must ensure that there is a register of qualified psychological service providers who meet the professional standards and ethics of the association, available on the internet, preferably with access from members' websites. »
« 4. Associations should consider developing advice for the public on psychological services and must make it available on the internet. This could include advice on criteria for choosing psychological and technical service providers, including factors such as adherence to human rights, security and content regulations, conflicts of interest, transparency and other issues. »

### 1.8. Research question and formulation of hypotheses
#### 1.8.1. Presentation of the request
Telepsychology, which encompasses the remote provision of psychotherapy and mental health services, is developing rapidly on an international scale.
13
---
This expanding field raises crucial questions concerning the quality of care, data security and the ethical standards governing these emerging practices.
Within the framework of this international study, we will explore the links between psychotherapists' sensitivity to the methodology of online practices and the recommendations from organisations, associations and federations in psychology in relation to the conscientiousness of their practices and their use of tools suited to online psychotherapy. This analysis will make it possible to highlight the methodological, ethical and ergonomic challenges and opportunities linked to the widespread adoption of telepsychology on an international scale.

#### 1.8.2. Objective of the study
The central research question of this study lies in the need to understand how telepsychologists choose their practice tool(s), how they integrate their methodology and the recommendations of professional organisations in relation to their sensitivity to ethics and the management of personal data in their online practice(s). It aims to highlight the key links that influence the effectiveness and compliance of telepsychology practices. By better understanding the attitudes and behaviours of psychotherapists towards recommendations and online methodologies, this study will help to identify needs in terms of continuing education and regulation to promote safe, effective and ethical telepsychology practices worldwide.
This study is essential to identify gaps and needs in terms of training, regulation and creation of ergonomic tools in this rapidly evolving field.
This research aims to inform efforts to optimise telepsychology practices on an international scale by providing empirical data on how psychotherapists integrate professional recommendations and best practices in their daily practice.

#### 1.8.3. Formulation of hypotheses
We formulate several hypotheses concerning the practices and perceptions of psychologists in the field of the ergonomics of communication tools as well as in their ethical and deontological sensitivity.
14
---
First, it is envisaged that psychologists encounter difficulties when evaluating the modalities of their ergonomic and communication tools. This perceived complexity could influence their choices regarding the tools they use in their professional practice.
Furthermore, it is assumed that despite the ethical and deontological framework, psychologists tend to favour popular, simple and practical tools. This inclination towards widespread and easy-to-use solutions may potentially lead them to compromise with respect to the ethical and deontological requirements of their profession.
Moreover, we anticipate positive correlations between several factors. It is assumed that there is a positive correlation between psychologists' perception of sufficient methodological training and their sensitivity to the management of personal data. Similarly, we envisage positive correlations between sensitivity to organisational recommendations for online practices, sensitivity to the ethical/deontological framework, and data management.
It is postulated that a growing number of psychologists observe that their patients express more concerns regarding data security compared to a previous study carried out by the EFPA in 2020.
Finally, we expect a correlation between the estimate of sufficient methodological training and a poor understanding of practice modalities via the telepractice professional's practice tool (Dunning-Kruger effect).

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1. Participants
We surveyed 370 international psychologists practising online or at a distance, recruited via invitations on professional platforms and professional directories.
Participants are of all ages; 50 participants have less than 5 years of professional experience and 132 participants have more than 20 years of professional experience. We asked them to answer our questions concerning their online practice modalities and concerning the use of their practice tools.
16
---
Data collection was carried out via a Google Forms questionnaire ensuring anonymity. The questionnaire included sections on demographic and professional information, tool use, and sensitivity to confidentiality and ethics questions.
Volunteer psychologists completed the online questionnaire, which took approximately four minutes. Before starting, they gave their consent to participate anonymously.

### 2.2. Procedure
Volunteer psychologists completed the online questionnaire, which took approximately three minutes to complete. Before starting, participants had to give their consent for the survey, confirming their anonymous participation. Data were collected via an online questionnaire designed on Google Forms. This questionnaire aims to evaluate the use and perception of communication and ergonomic tools in telepsychology, with a particular emphasis on data security, compliance with ethical standards and practical effectiveness.
The data collected via the questionnaire were then used to analyse online practices and their correlation with various professional and personal factors. The data were analysed with descriptive statistics and statistical tests to evaluate the relationships between different variables. The study was conducted in accordance with ethical principles, ensuring respect for the confidentiality and anonymity of participants, as well as ethical use of the data collected. The methodology included an online questionnaire aimed at evaluating the use and perception of tools in telepsychology, with an emphasis on data security and ethical effectiveness. Items were associated with different hypotheses to test psychologists' perceptions regarding the security, ethics and effectiveness of the tools used. This detailed methodology ensures a rigorous analysis of psychologists' perceptions, using specific items to test each hypothesis and guarantee consistency in the measurement of perceptions and behaviours.
The data were analysed to evaluate the relationships between the different variables using descriptive statistics, Pearson correlation tests, chi-square tests, and analysis of variance (ANOVA) for the different hypotheses tested. These analyses helped to identify patterns of tool use, as well as perceptions and sensitivities regarding online practices among psychologists.

## 4. Discussion of results according to the hypotheses formulated

### Hypothesis 1: Data security and characteristics of tools
The analysis of the results for Hypothesis 1 reveals that psychologists evaluate various communication tools on the basis of the number of incorrect answers they gave regarding the security and confidentiality characteristics of the tools. Low scores indicate a higher number of incorrect answers, highlighting significant misunderstandings regarding the data security offered by the tools. The results show that more specialised tools such as service platforms tend to be rated more favourably, while more general tools, such as social networks, show insufficient scores, indicating a less favourable perception of their security and confidentiality characteristics.
This tendency to evaluate tools differently according to their specific field of application could reflect variability in the way tools are presented to psychology professionals, as well as in their understanding of the implications of these tools for professional practice. The results suggest a critical need to improve data security training for psychologists, especially regarding tools that are not traditionally associated with highly regulated or sensitive contexts. Better training could contribute to a more uniform understanding and a more adequate evaluation of technologies, which is essential to guarantee the protection of client data.
27
---
Continuing education in data security should be a priority for trainers and professional organisations in psychology. This includes not only regular training sessions but also constant updates on best practices and new threats in data security. It is also essential that psychologists be trained to evaluate critically the tools they choose to use, understanding not only the functional benefits of these tools but also their potential implications for the confidentiality and security of client information. Certification programmes could integrate specific modules on the evaluation of mental health technologies, and professional associations could develop clear guidelines to help their members make informed choices regarding technology.

**Hypothesis 2: Popularity vs ethical constraints**
The very weak negative correlation (r = -0.059, p = 0.869) indicates that psychologists who choose tools for their popularity do not report ethical constraints significantly, suggesting a possible underestimation or ignorance of the ethical implications linked to these tools.
The non-significance of the results suggests that the popularity of a tool can often mask underlying problems linked to confidentiality and security that professionals might neglect. This tendency to prefer popular tools without in-depth evaluation of their compliance with ethical standards can expose users and their clients to unanticipated risks. It is crucial to introduce targeted training that encourages professionals to carry out critical evaluations of the tools they choose to use, emphasising the importance of data confidentiality and security beyond mere popularity.
28
---
The results underline the need for trainers, institutions and professional associations to strengthen the education and training of psychologists in data security. This includes not only technical training on secure communication tools but also guidance on risk management and response to security incidents. Clear and accessible guidelines on best practices in data security should be developed and regularly updated to respond to technological developments and new threats. Psychologists must be equipped not only to address their own concerns but also to reassure their patients about the protection of their privacy.

**Hypothesis 3: Simplicity and ethical constraints**
The very weak non-significant negative correlation (r = -0.067, p = 0.902) suggests that choices based on the simplicity of use of tools are not strongly influenced by ethical considerations, which could indicate a prioritisation of operational efficiency over ethical standards.
This tendency to value simplicity could reflect a need for convenience or efficiency that prevails over ethical concerns, possibly due to a lack of awareness of the implications of these choices. It is vital to strengthen continuing education in ethics applied to technology to help professionals understand that simplicity must not come at the expense of data security and confidentiality. The integration of training modules on the ethical evaluation of technological tools into initial and continuing training programmes could help to fill this gap.
It is essential to develop awareness campaigns and workshops for mental health professionals, emphasising the importance of ethical considerations when choosing practical tools. These programmes should aim to increase knowledge of the potential implications of the uncritical use of technological tools and to promote in-depth ethical reflection in the technological decision-making process. In parallel, the integration of case discussions and concrete examples in continuing education could help to illustrate the potential dangers of neglecting ethical aspects in daily practice.
29
---
**Hypothesis 4: Practicality vs ethical constraints**
The extremely weak and non-significant correlation (r = 0.022, p = 0.336) shows that psychologists who choose tools for their practicality do not report ethical constraints significantly. This observation suggests that practicality may take precedence over ethical considerations, or that users do not perceive ethical implications as relevant to their choice of practical tools.
The preference for practicality without adequate consideration of ethical aspects poses a potential risk, especially in sensitive fields such as psychology where client data confidentiality and security are paramount. This tendency to neglect ethical constraints may be due to insufficient training on the risks associated with the uncritical use of practical but potentially insecure technologies.
It is essential to develop awareness campaigns and workshops for mental health professionals, emphasising the importance of ethical considerations when choosing practical tools. These programmes should aim to increase knowledge of the potential implications of the uncritical use of technological tools and to promote in-depth ethical reflection in the technological decision-making process.

**Hypothesis 5: Training and sensitivity to data management**
The moderate and significant correlation (r = 0.367, p < 0.001) indicates that psychologists who perceive that they have been sufficiently trained methodologically in the confidentiality and management of their patients' personal data are likely to report greater sensitivity to the management of this data.
This correlation shows the importance of training in strengthening sensitivity to good data management practices. The significant link suggests that adequate training programmes can effectively increase professionals' awareness and competence in confidentiality and data security.

Psychology training programmes should integrate modules on personal data management, emphasising the importance of this competence in daily practice. Moreover, it would be useful for professional associations to develop guidelines and continuing training to maintain and strengthen sensitivity to personal data management among psychologists.

**Hypothesis 6: Sensitivity to recommendations and personal data management**
The significant correlation (r = 0.261, p < 0.001) between sensitivity to organisational recommendations for online practices and sensitivity to personal data management shows that psychologists who take into account and actively follow the recommendations of professional associations also show greater attention and application in the management of their patients' personal data.
These results could reflect an important distinction between theoretical knowledge of recommendations and the practical application of data management. Although psychologists may be aware of and value ethical recommendations, it is their daily, practical attention to data management that translates into better compliance.
The results underline the importance of psychology training focusing not only on teaching ethical standards but also on practical applications concerning data management. Training institutions should consider practical modules that allow psychologists to put their knowledge of confidentiality and data security into practice. Moreover, professional associations could develop tools and resources to help members apply recommendations effectively in their daily practice.

**Hypothesis 7: Sensitivity to the ethical/deontological framework and data management**
The significant positive correlation (r = 0.203, p < 0.001) shows that psychologists who are attentive to the ethical and deontological aspects of their practice are also more sensitive to appropriate management of personal data. This indicates that the consideration of ethical frameworks is integrated into the way data are managed on a daily basis.

This may reflect a deep integration of ethical values into all facets of their professional practice, suggesting that those who value ethical principles are also more vigilant in the protection of patient information. It is essential to maintain a high level of ethical awareness to guarantee compliance with best practices in confidentiality and security.
Strengthening ethical training could therefore have positive effects on data management. Training institutions and professional associations should consider integrating in-depth ethical modules that specifically address data management, as well as promoting an ongoing dialogue on the importance of ethics in modern psychological practice.

**Hypothesis 8: Years of practice and sensitivity to data management**
The non-significant correlation (r = -0.017, p = 0.370) suggests that the experience accumulated by psychologists does not directly affect their sensitivity to the management of personal data. This indicates that the length of professional experience does not guarantee better data management.
This could indicate that other factors, such as continuing education or personal adaptation to technological changes, play a more crucial role in psychologists' ability to manage personal data effectively. It is possible that more experienced practitioners do not update their data protection skills as regularly as they should.
The results underline the need for training bodies and professional associations to provide continuing training and regular updates on best practices in data protection, regardless of practitioners' seniority.

**Hypothesis 9: Sensitivity to data management and years of practice**
The very weak and non-significant correlation (r = 0.086, p = 0.952) shows that the number of years of practice is not associated with a better perception of sensitivity to personal data management.
This absence of correlation reinforces the idea that professional experience in itself is not sufficient to ensure effective management of personal data. This suggests that factors such as specific continuing education in data protection are essential to maintain and improve the skills necessary in this field.
Providing continuing training resources to all psychologists, regardless of their experience, is crucial to guarantee effective management of personal data. Professional organisations should promote regular updates and specialised training in data protection.

**Hypothesis 10: Increase in psychologists' concerns regarding data security since 2020**
The statistical analysis indicates that more psychologists express concerns related to data security in comparison with the 2020 study carried out by the EFPA. The χ² goodness-of-fit test (χ² = 7.14, df = 1, p = 0.008) suggests that this increase is statistically significant, reflecting a growing awareness of the risks associated with data security in a context of telepsychology and increased use of digital tools.
This increase in concerns can be attributed to several factors, including the growing prevalence of cyberattacks and data breaches, as well as more frequent public and professional discussion of these topics. This indicates increased awareness but also possible anxiety among psychologists about their ability to protect their patients' sensitive information effectively.

**Hypothesis 11: Sufficient methodological training and interpretation of the modalities of the tool used**
The presence of a significant correlation (F = 7.99, p = 0.005) suggests that psychologists who perceive themselves as well trained in data security may in reality have lower performance, which is indicative of the Dunning-Kruger effect.
This result highlights a potential problem of overconfidence among psychologists who believe they have mastered data security, which can lead to practical errors in the management of sensitive data. The Dunning-Kruger effect, where individuals with limited competence in a domain overestimate their abilities, seems to play a role here, which can lead to insufficient application of security best practices.
It is crucial to develop objective assessments to measure psychologists' real competence in data security, rather than relying solely on their self-assessment. Regular training and skills assessments could help to mitigate this overconfidence effect and guarantee safer management of sensitive data.

### Research limitations
It is important to highlight the potential methodological limitations linked to this study. The methodology applied to this research thesis may have limitations in connection with the size of our sample (N = 370), which could be considered too limited, or by the fact that this sample contains more German participants (N = 189) than Russian participants (N = 25), for example. Our measurement methods (online questionnaire) could also have limitations, in particular for participants who encountered potential comprehension difficulties. The questionnaire being administered at a distance, it is possible that some participants lacked rigour and/or sincerity in their responses. Despite our efforts to guarantee the validity and reliability of the study, these limitations may have influenced the validity and/or reliability of certain results and/or conclusions of the study. We have however obtained very reliable reliability scores (df) (see section 4.).
39
---
Conceptual limitations could also exist; concepts or theories that could have strengthened the validity of the research may have been omitted. These conceptual limitations could restrict the scope of our interpretations and/or our conclusions.
Regarding data-related considerations, a larger and more distributed sample across the different countries of the participants could increase the validity of the study. An approach by country, by geographical area and/or by association/organisation could also be relevant to analyse more precisely the practice of psychologists in relation to their relationship to methodology, deontology and data management/security.
